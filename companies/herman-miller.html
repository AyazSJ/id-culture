About

Herman Miller is an American furniture company founded in Zeeland, Michigan in 1905 that became, through a series of extraordinarily consequential design partnerships, one of the most influential furniture manufacturers of the twentieth century and the company most closely associated with the American midcentury design canon. The Herman Miller name is inseparable from the Eames lounge chair, the Aeron chair, George Nelson's bubble lamps, and the Action Office — objects that between them span the full arc of postwar American design.

The company was originally a conventional furniture manufacturer producing period-style reproductions. Its transformation began in 1930 when Gilbert Rohde persuaded D.J. De Pree, the company's president and a man of genuine intellectual curiosity, that the future lay in modern design rather than historical pastiche. Rohde's decade of work for Herman Miller established the company's commitment to contemporary design and its willingness to take commercial risks on serious designers. After Rohde's death in 1944, De Pree made the appointment that would define the company's identity: George Nelson as design director, with the brief to continue the modern direction and the freedom to bring in other designers. Nelson's first act was to introduce De Pree to Charles and Ray Eames.

The subsequent Herman Miller design programme — the Eames plywood and fibreglass chairs, Nelson's storage systems and clocks, Isamu Noguchi's table, Alexander Girard's textile programme, Bob Propst's Action Office — was the most sustained and consistently excellent corporate design programme in American furniture history. The Aeron chair (1994, by Bill Stumpf and Don Chadwick) extended the tradition into the ergonomic era and became the most commercially successful chair in the company's history. Herman Miller merged with Knoll in 2021 to form MillerKnoll.
