About

Allgemeine Elektricitäts-Gesellschaft (AEG), founded in Berlin in 1883 by Emil Rathenau, was one of Germany's largest and most important electrical engineering companies and the site of the world's first comprehensive corporate design program. When Peter Behrens was appointed artistic director in 1907, he became responsible not just for product design but for architecture, graphics, typography, advertising, and the overall visual identity of the company — an unprecedented brief that established the template for what we now call brand identity.

Behrens's AEG is a landmark in design history for several reasons. It demonstrated that a single coherent design vision could be applied consistently across an entire company's output — from factory buildings to electric kettles to product catalogues. It showed that design was a strategic business tool, not a finishing touch. And it produced the first generation of what we might call corporate designers, with Behrens's Berlin office serving as a training ground for Le Corbusier, Mies van der Rohe, and Walter Gropius simultaneously in the years around 1910.

Behrens's product designs for AEG — electric fans, arc lamps, kettles, clocks — were distinguished by their geometric clarity, structural honesty, and systematic approach to family relationships between products. His AEG typeface (1908) and the hexagonal AEG logo were among the earliest examples of a coherent corporate typographic identity.

AEG remained a major German industrial conglomerate through the twentieth century, diversifying into power generation, transportation, and telecommunications before its eventual breakup and partial acquisition by various companies in the 1990s and 2000s. The brand is now owned by Electrolux.
