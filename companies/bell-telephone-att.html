About

Bell Telephone Company, founded by Alexander Graham Bell in 1877 and eventually evolving into AT&T, is significant in design history not just as the company that commercialized the telephone but as one of the earliest and most sustained corporate patrons of industrial design. Bell's long relationship with Henry Dreyfuss produced some of the most carefully human-factored and widely used objects of the twentieth century.

The early telephone was a purely functional instrument with no designed aesthetic — a transmitter, receiver, and mechanism housed in whatever form was expedient. By the 1920s, as the telephone moved from novelty to domestic necessity, Bell began to think about how the instrument should look and feel in American homes. The resulting design program, which engaged Henry Dreyfuss from 1930 onward, produced a series of handsets that defined what a telephone looked like for generations.

Dreyfuss's Model 302 (1937) was the first telephone designed as a unified sculptural form — handset and base integrated into a single coherent object rather than assembled from separate components. His Model 500 (1950) refined this further, becoming the definitive American telephone of the postwar era. Dreyfuss and his team conducted extensive ergonomic research to ensure the handset fit the human hand and face correctly — an early example of systematic human factors research in consumer product design.

AT&T's later Trimline phone (1965) took the logic further, moving the dial into the handset itself. The succession of Bell telephone designs constitutes one of the clearest case studies in how a product can evolve through sustained design investment from utilitarian artifact to carefully considered object.
