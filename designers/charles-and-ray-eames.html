About

Charles and Ray Eames were the most comprehensively influential American designers of the twentieth century — a husband-and-wife partnership whose output across furniture, film, exhibition design, architecture, toys, and graphic communication defined the optimistic visual and material language of postwar America. Their central conviction — that good design should be simultaneously beautiful, honest, and accessible to ordinary people — ran through everything they made.

Charles (1907–1978) trained as an architect in St. Louis; Ray Kaiser (1912–1988) studied painting under Hans Hofmann in New York. They met at the Cranbrook Academy of Art in Michigan in 1940, married in 1941, and moved to California where they set up a studio that would eventually occupy a converted warehouse on Washington Boulevard in Venice. Their early experiments with molded plywood — begun during the war when they produced lightweight splints and stretchers for the US Navy — led directly to their landmark furniture: the LCW and DCW chairs (1946), which achieved compound-curve three-dimensional bends in plywood that Aalto's simpler single-direction bends had not.

Their relationship with Herman Miller, which began in 1946, produced not just individual products but a new model for how a furniture company and a design office could work together over decades. The Fiberglass Shell Chair (1950) — the first chair mass-produced in plastic — was followed by the Lounge Chair and Ottoman (1956), the Aluminum Group (1958), the Soft Pad Group (1969), and dozens more. Each represented a systematic exploration of a material and manufacturing process pushed to its formal and functional limit. Beyond furniture, the Eameses produced over 125 short films — including the nine-minute Powers of Ten (1977), a journey from the scale of the universe to the scale of a proton — designed the IBM Pavilion at the 1964 World's Fair, and built their own home from standard industrial components (Case Study House No. 8, 1949).
