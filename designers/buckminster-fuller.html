About

R. Buckminster Fuller was an American architect, systems theorist, inventor, and futurist whose ideas about design as a means to "do more with less" — what he called ephemeralization — made him one of the most visionary and idiosyncratic figures in twentieth-century design history. Fuller approached design not as the decoration of objects but as the strategic reorganization of the physical world's resources for maximum human benefit.

After failing out of Harvard twice and experiencing a personal crisis in 1927 that brought him to the brink of suicide, Fuller committed the rest of his life to an experiment: could one individual make a meaningful difference in the world through design thinking alone? He called himself "Guinea Pig B" and began working on what he considered the most fundamental design problems — shelter, transportation, energy, and resource efficiency.

His geodesic dome, for which he received a patent in 1954, became his most successful invention: a spherical structure assembled from triangular elements that achieves extraordinary structural efficiency, enclosing the maximum volume with the minimum surface area and material. Geodesic domes were adopted by the US military for radar installations, by the counterculture movement for communal living, and by institutions from Epcot (the biosphere in Montreal is a Fuller dome) to science research stations in Antarctica.

His Dymaxion projects — the Dymaxion House (1928, a mass-producible hexagonal dwelling suspended from a central mast), the Dymaxion Car (1933, a three-wheeled aerodynamic vehicle), and the Dymaxion World Map (1943, a projection that minimized distortion) — were ambitious attempts to rethink familiar typologies from first principles. Most were commercial failures but intellectual provocations that influenced later designers profoundly.

Fuller lectured ceaselessly, wrote prolifically (coining terms like "Spaceship Earth," "synergetics," and "tensegrity"), and influenced a generation of architects, engineers, and designers to think systemically about the planet's resources.
