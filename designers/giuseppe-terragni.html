About

Giuseppe Terragni was an Italian architect and designer who is considered the foremost representative of Italian Rationalism — the Modernist architectural movement that developed in Italy during the 1920s and 1930s as a synthesis of the International Style's formal rigour with a distinctly Italian clarity and material refinement. His Casa del Fascio in Como (1936) is one of the finest and most perfectly resolved buildings of the entire Modernist period, a work of such geometric precision and spatial intelligence that it remains a touchstone for architects nearly a century after its completion.

Terragni studied at the Milan Polytechnic and co-founded Gruppo 7, the Italian rationalist architectural group, in 1926. He worked primarily in Como, where his small but exceptional body of work — the Novocomum apartment block (1929), the Asilo Sant'Elia kindergarten (1937), and above all the Casa del Fascio — demonstrated an Italian approach to Modernism that was warmer, more materially refined, and more attentive to classical proportion than the northern European mainstream.

His furniture design is less well known than his architecture but shares the same qualities: the Follia chair (1934), produced for the Casa del Fascio interiors, is a tubular steel piece of exceptional formal elegance — simpler and more resolved than most contemporary Italian tubular steel furniture, with a lightness that makes it feel almost drawn in space rather than constructed. Terragni died young, in 1943, after a mental breakdown following his service on the Eastern Front during the Second World War. His architecture has been the subject of sustained critical attention from architects including Peter Eisenman, who devoted much of his early theoretical work to analysis of the Casa del Fascio.
