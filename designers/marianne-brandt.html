About

Marianne Brandt was a German artist and designer who was one of the most talented students the Bauhaus produced and the only woman to have led a Bauhaus workshop — the metal workshop, which she directed from 1928 to 1929 after László Moholy-Nagy's departure. Her tea and coffee services, ashtrays, lamps, and other metalwork objects are among the most formally elegant and historically significant products of the Bauhaus era.

Born in Chemnitz, Brandt studied painting before joining the Bauhaus in 1923, initially in the weaving workshop (where women students were typically directed regardless of their interests) before making her way into the metal workshop under Moholy-Nagy's influence. Her early metalwork explored the formal possibilities of geometric abstraction in silver and brass: tea caddies and infusers that reduced domestic objects to their essential geometric forms — spheres, cylinders, hemispheres — with a precision and material quality that made them look like sculptures as much as functional objects.

Her most commercially successful design was the Kandem bedside lamp (1928), produced in collaboration with Hin Bredendieck for Körting & Mathiesen, which became the Bauhaus's best-selling licensed product. But her most celebrated single object is the MT 49 tea infuser (1924) — a hemispherical form in silver that sits on a small tray and swings open to deposit loose tea — which is now one of the most reproduced and collected Bauhaus objects in existence. After leaving the Bauhaus, Brandt worked briefly at the Ruppelwerk metalware company before returning to Germany and eventually to a teaching post at the Berlin Academy of Arts, where she worked until retirement.
