About

Lurelle Guild was an American industrial designer who was one of the first generation of professional industrial designers in the United States, working during the 1930s and 1940s on a wide range of consumer products that brought the streamlined aesthetic of the period to the everyday domestic market. His work for Wear-Ever Aluminum — a range of kitchenware products that are among the most elegantly designed aluminum objects of the Art Deco period — represents his most sustained and celebrated achievement.

Guild established himself in New York in the late 1920s and built a broad-based practice encompassing homegoods, packaging, vehicles, and product design. His approach combined the Streamlining vocabulary of the period — smooth surfaces, horizontal speed lines, rounded corners — with a practical understanding of manufacturing economics and the requirements of the domestic market. His aluminum cookware and kitchenware for Wear-Ever, designed in the early 1930s, achieved the rare combination of genuine formal elegance and commercial accessibility that the best American industrial design of the period aspired to.

Guild was also a significant figure in the early institutionalization of industrial design as a profession, helping to found the American Designers' Institute and to establish the professional and ethical standards of the emerging field. He wrote one of the first textbooks on industrial design practice, and his advocacy for the profession's recognition as a legitimate discipline alongside architecture and engineering contributed to the establishment of industrial design education in American universities.
