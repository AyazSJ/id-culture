About

Raymond Loewy was a French-American industrial designer who was for decades the most famous designer in the world — the man who "streamlined the sales curve" of American industry, whose face appeared on the cover of Time magazine in 1949, and whose career from the 1920s to the 1970s touched virtually every category of designed object from locomotives to lip balm. His MAYA principle — Most Advanced Yet Acceptable — remains one of the most quoted and practically useful maxims in design thinking.

Born in Paris, Loewy studied engineering before moving to New York in 1919 with virtually no money and building a career first in fashion illustration and window display before pivoting to industrial design in the late 1920s. His redesign of the Gestetner duplicating machine in 1929 — his first major industrial design commission, secured by simply showing up at Gestetner's New York offices with a clay model of what the machine could look like — launched a practice that would eventually employ over 150 people and work simultaneously for Studebaker, the Pennsylvania Railroad, Lucky Strike cigarettes, Sears Roebuck, Shell, Coca-Cola, NASA, and dozens of other major clients.

Loewy's formal approach was pragmatic rather than ideological: he did not subscribe to the moral functionalism of the Bauhaus or the futurism of Bel Geddes but simply sought to make objects better-looking and more commercially successful, believing that appearance and sales performance were directly connected. His Studebaker automobiles of the early 1950s — particularly the 1953 Starliner coupe — were among the most beautiful American cars of the postwar period. His Pennsylvania Railroad GG-1 locomotive (1934) and S-1 locomotive (1939) set the visual standard for American railway power. His Lucky Strike cigarette package redesign (1942) — simply reversing the background from green to white, saving green ink and allowing the red target to dominate — was produced without a single client meeting and became one of the most celebrated packaging case studies in design history.
