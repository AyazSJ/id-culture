About

Hector Guimard was a French architect and designer who is best known for the extraordinary cast iron entrance structures he designed for the Paris Métro between 1900 and 1913 — the dragon-headed lamp standards, sinuous railings, and organic canopy supports that became the most visible and widely reproduced examples of French Art Nouveau and that gave the style one of its popular names: style Métro or style Guimard.

Born in Lyon, Guimard trained at the École des Arts Décoratifs and the École des Beaux-Arts in Paris before travelling to Brussels, where a meeting with Victor Horta in 1895 was decisive. Horta's Tassel House (1893) showed Guimard what Art Nouveau architecture could achieve in three dimensions — how the organic line could be extended from surface ornament into structural form itself. Guimard returned to Paris and applied these insights to his own work with an energy and formal invention that surpassed even Horta's.

His Castel Béranger apartment building in Paris (1898), with its extraordinary variety of organic ornament on every surface, was the building that established his reputation and won him the commission for the Métro entrances. The Métro structures — produced in standardized cast iron sections that could be assembled in different configurations at different stations — are a remarkable synthesis of industrially produced standard components and organic visual language: they are mass-produced Art Nouveau, achieving their distinctive character through the design of the individual parts rather than bespoke fabrication. Most were demolished in the 1920s and 1930s as the style fell out of favour; those that survive — including the landmark entrance at Abbesses — are now protected as historic monuments.
