About

Jean Heiberg and B.C. Bjørklund were the Norwegian designers responsible for the Ericsson DBH 1001 telephone (1931), one of the most significant telephone designs of the twentieth century and the model that established the form of the combined handset telephone — the design that united transmitter and receiver into a single handheld unit — as the standard for decades of subsequent telephone design.

Heiberg was a Norwegian painter and sculptor with no prior industrial design experience when Ericsson engaged him to design a new telephone. Working with Bjørklund, an Ericsson engineer, he produced a design of remarkable formal clarity: the cradle and handset conceived as a unified sculptural object in Bakelite, with a dial that integrated naturally into the base and a handset whose ergonomic logic — both earpiece and mouthpiece angled to align naturally with the face — was arrived at through empirical testing. The resulting form was so well resolved that it essentially defined the shape of the telephone handset for the following thirty years.

Henry Dreyfuss's Bell Model 302 (1937) — itself the design that most directly established the American telephone aesthetic — drew significantly on the Heiberg-Bjørklund precedent. The DBH 1001 is therefore a founding document of the modern telephone's form: a design that solved the ergonomic and formal problems of the combined handset so effectively that its successors were primarily refinements rather than reinventions.
